Overview

Robotics foundation models are rapidly transforming robot learning, yet the field lacks consensus on a fundamental question: what pre-training objectives actually matter? Today's most capable systems — including Vision-Language-Action models (VLAs), World Action Models (WAMs), and related architectures — draw on heterogeneous data sources spanning internet-scale video, large-scale simulation, and real robot demonstrations. Each modality implies a different supervisory signal: behavior cloning from teleoperation, action-conditioned future prediction, latent action inference from passive observation, and more. These objectives are rarely compared directly, and the choices that dominate today's systems often reflect data availability and engineering convenience as much as a principled understanding of what embodied intelligence requires.

Several tensions sit at the heart of this uncertainty. Can action grounding be learned from passive internet video, where action labels are absent, or is large-scale interaction data indispensable? Do generative world models yield representations that transfer better than those learned by directly imitating actions? At what level of abstraction should policies be supervised — latent actions, end-effector trajectories, or low-level control? How much does natural language contribute to physical capability versus serving as a convenient task interface? And do the scaling laws that govern language and vision pre-training even hold for robotics, where data is scarcer, more expensive, and far less uniform?

This half-day workshop brings together researchers from robot learning, computer vision, and foundation model development to surface and discuss the core principles that should guide pre-training for embodied AI. Rather than showcasing incremental system improvements, the workshop foregrounds disagreement: we invite contrarian positions, negative results, and cross-cutting analyses that clarify why particular objectives succeed or fail. Through invited talks, contributed papers, and a panel discussion, we aim to move the community toward a clearer account of what pre-training for robotics should optimize for.

The workshop targets researchers and practitioners working on robot manipulation, locomotion, and embodied AI broadly, as well as those from adjacent fields such as computer vision, natural language processing, and generative modeling. We particularly welcome participants who can bring evidence and perspective from outside robotics proper, since many of the pre-training objectives now under debate originated in these neighboring communities.

Core Challenges & Research Questions

The workshop is organized around six open questions about pre-training for robotics.

  • What pre-training objectives transfer best to downstream robot tasks?

    Does next-token prediction over video, action-conditioned prediction, contrastive alignment, or some combination yield representations that generalize to unseen manipulation and locomotion tasks?

  • How should models balance passive video vs. active interaction data?

    Internet-scale video is cheap and abundant but passive; real robot data is scarce but action-grounded. What is the right mixture and objective design to exploit both?

  • What role does simulation play in pre-training?

    Simulation offers unlimited interaction data but suffers from a reality gap. How can pre-training objectives be designed to minimize sim-to-real transfer costs?

  • VLAs vs. World Action Models vs. alternatives — what does each get right?

    How do language-conditioned action prediction objectives compare to world-model-based objectives that predict future states? What are the failure modes of each?

  • How do we evaluate pre-training quality?

    What benchmarks and probing tasks can reliably measure whether a pre-trained model has acquired the physical common sense, object permanence, and causal understanding robots need?

  • Do scaling laws hold for robotics pre-training?

    Do the scaling laws established for language and vision transfer to robot learning? How does data diversity interact with model scale under different objectives?

Call for Papers

We solicit 8-page workshop papers (plus references), formatted using the CoRL template. The workshop is non-archival. Topics include but are not limited to:

  • Novel pre-training objectives for robot learning (video prediction, action-conditioned modeling, contrastive methods, etc.)
  • Comparative evaluations of VLAs, World Action Models, and related architectures
  • Data curation and mixture strategies for robot foundation model pre-training
  • Sim-to-real transfer under different pre-training regimes
  • Scaling laws and evaluation benchmarks for embodied pre-training
  • Theoretical analyses of what different objectives capture about the physical world

Submissions will be reviewed by the organizers and program committee for relevance and quality. Accepted papers will be posted on this website. Two Best Paper Awards with a sponsored prize will be presented to the most outstanding submissions; an author from each awarded paper will be invited to join the panel discussion.
